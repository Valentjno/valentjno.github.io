Red-Teaming as Responsible AI Practice

Red-teaming is not just a security exercise—it's a fundamental pillar of Responsible AI. By systematically identifying vulnerabilities, edge cases, and failure modes before systems reach production, red-teaming ensures AI systems are more secure, predictable, and aligned with organizational values.

My work applies adversarial thinking to every stage of AI development: from initial model evaluation through deployment and monitoring. I develop methodologies that help organizations understand what can go wrong and how to build more resilient systems.

Responsible AI Through Security

Red-teaming advances Responsible AI by making abstract safety principles concrete and measurable. When we identify a prompt injection vulnerability, test robustness across diverse inputs, or evaluate how a model handles adversarial edge cases, we're not just finding bugs—we're building evidence that systems behave as intended and can be trusted in production.

Security research provides the rigorous methodology, empirical evidence, and systematic frameworks that Responsible AI requires. It transforms good intentions into actionable security practices.

Red-Teaming Methodologies

Threat Modeling

Systematic identification of attack surfaces, adversary motivations, and potential exploit paths. Creates structured risk matrices to prioritize security work based on likelihood and impact.

Adversarial Testing

Rigorous evaluation of model robustness through adversarial inputs, prompt injections, jailbreak attempts, and adversarial examples. Tests both targeted attacks and generalized failure modes.

Security Frameworks

Application of established security principles—defense in depth, least privilege, secure by default—to AI system architecture and deployment. Translates theoretical security into practical implementation.

Responsible AI Integration

Embedding security evaluation into responsible AI workflows: fairness testing, bias detection, transparency assessment, and alignment verification. Ensures security and ethics develop together, not in isolation.
